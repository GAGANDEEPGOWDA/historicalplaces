INDIAN KINGS AND THEIR PASSION TOWARDS CREATING MONUMENTS

Indian kings throughout history exhibited a profound passion for creating magnificent monuments that showcased their power, wealth, and artistic sensibilities. The Taj Mahal, built by Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan as a tribute to his beloved wife, is a testament to this passion. Constructed with exquisite white marble and adorned with intricate carvings and inlays, it stands as a symbol of eternal love and architectural grandeur. Other kings, such as the Chola rulers in South India and the Rajputs in Rajasthan, also left behind a rich legacy of temples, palaces, and fortresses that exemplify their dedication to architectural excellence and the patronage of the arts. These awe-inspiring monuments continue to captivate the world with their beauty and historical significance








HERE ARE SOME OF THE EXAMPLES OF FAMOUS EMPERORS WHO HAD CONTRIBUTED FOR THE HISTORICAL INDIA.

Emperor Shah Jahan

(reigned 1628-1658)

Shah Jahan, a Mughal emperor, is renowned for constructing the Taj Mahal in Agra, Uttar Pradesh.

Emperor Akbar

(reigned 1556-1605)

He established the city of Fatehpur Sikri, which houses magnificent structures like the Buland Darwaza, Jama Masjid, and Panch Mahal.

Emperor Ashoka

(reigned 268-232 BCE)

Ashoka Pillar at Sarnath, near Varanasi, which bears inscriptions related to Ashoka's teachings and philosophy.

King Krishnadevaraya

(reigned 1509-1529)

Under his patronage, iconic structures like the Vittala Temple, Hazara Rama Temple, and the King's Balance were built in the capital city of Hampi.

King Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj

(reigned 1674-1680)

He constructed numerous forts across Maharashtra, including the famous Raigad Fort, which served as his capital.

King Raja Raja Chola

(reigned 985-1014)

The Brihadeeswarar Temple in Thanjavur, dedicated to Lord Shiva, is his most celebrated creation and is recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage site..


These are just a few examples of kings who contributed significantly to the creation of historical monuments in India. Their architectural endeavors have left a lasting impact and continue to be admired for their grandeur and cultural significance.

RAJASTHAN

Known as the "Land of Kings," Rajasthan is famous for its majestic forts, palaces, and havelis. Some prominent historical monuments in Rajasthan include Amber Fort, Mehrangarh Fort, City Palace Jaipur, Umaid Bhawan Palace, and Hawa Mahal.

The state of Uttar Pradesh is renowned for its historical significance. It is home to iconic monuments like the Taj Mahal in Agra, Agra Fort, Fatehpur Sikri, and the magnificent temples of Varanasi.

Maharashtra

Maharashtra boasts several historical monuments, including the famous Ajanta and Ellora Caves, which are UNESCO World Heritage Sites. Other notable monuments include the Gateway of India and Elephanta Caves in Mumbai, and the Bibi Ka Maqbara in Aurangabad.

Tamil Nadu

With a rich cultural heritage, Tamil Nadu houses ancient temples and architectural wonders. Prominent historical monuments in the state include the Brihadeeswarar Temple in Thanjavur, Meenakshi Amman Temple in Madurai, Shore Temple in Mahabalipuram, and the Great Living Chola Temples.

Madhya Pradesh

Madhya Pradesh is known for its magnificent historical sites such as Khajuraho Group of Monuments, Sanchi Stupa, Gwalior Fort, and Mandu Fort. The state is also home to the UNESCO World Heritage Site of Bhimbetka Rock Shelters.

Karnataka

Karnataka boasts a rich heritage and is home to the UNESCO World Heritage Sites of Hampi, Pattadakal, and the group of monuments at Badami. Other notable historical monuments in Karnataka include Mysore Palace, Gol Gumbaz in Bijapur, and Belur and Halebidu temples.

Gujarat

Gujarat is known for its historical and architectural treasures such as the Rani Ki Vav stepwell in Patan, Champaner-Pavagadh Archaeological Park, Sun Temple in Modhera, and the Sabarmati Ashram in Ahmedabad.

Andhra Pradesh

In Andhra Pradesh, there are several historical monuments that have been recognized as Monuments of National Importance by the Archaeological Survey of India (ASI).

Bihar

The state of Bihar in India is blessed with a rich heritage of historical monuments that reflect its cultural and architectural grandeur. These monuments stand as testaments to the region's glorious past and attract visitors from around the world.






GOVERNMENT CONCERN TOWARDS PROTECTING HISTORICAL MONUMENTS


  • The Ministry of Culture in India is responsible for promoting and preserving India's rich cultural heritage and supporting various art forms across the country. The ministry focuses on initiatives to safeguard and promote India's diverse cultural traditions, art, literature, music, dance, theater, and more.
  • The Indian government is deeply concerned about preserving the historical monuments of India due to their cultural significance and the need to protect the country's rich heritage. India has introduced strict laws, such as the Antiquities and Art Treasures Act of 1972 (amended in 1976) , to prevent the illicit export of archaeological objects and enforce state ownership of such artifacts
  • However, the implementation of these laws remains a challenge, primarily due to poverty-driven looting and inadequate security measures
  • The Archaeological Survey of India (ASI), responsible for protecting and preserving over 3,650 monuments, faces a lack of enforcement due to insufficient staffing, hindering the safeguarding of historic sites.
  • The government recognizes the need for heritage education, imparting historical knowledge in schools, and involving local politicians to tighten vigilance and control trafficking agents at the grassroots level.
  • By addressing these concerns, the Indian government aims to protect its historical monuments and preserve the country's rich cultural legacy.

Mysore Palace, Karnataka:

(Known for its dazzling royal heritage and intricate architecture, Mysore Palace is one of the most significant historical monuments in South India.)

  • Mysore Palace, also known as Amba Vilas Palace, is a magnificent historical palace and royal residence located in Mysore, Karnataka, India. It served as the official residence of the Wadiyar dynasty and the seat of the Kingdom of Mysore. Situated in the heart of Mysore, the palace faces the majestic Chamundi Hills in the east. Known as the "City of Palaces," Mysore boasts seven palaces, with the Mysore Palace being the most renowned and iconic
  • The construction of the Mysore Palace dates back to the late 19th century. The original palace, built within the Old Fort in the 14th century, was destroyed by fire multiple times. Consequently, the new palace, made of stone, bricks, and wood, was constructed between 1897 and 1912 under the guidance of British architect Henry Irwin. This palace, often referred to as the New Fort, replaced the previous wooden structure and became the residence of the royal family.
  • The architectural design of Mysore Palace is an exquisite blend of various styles, including Indo-Saracenic, Rajput, and Gothic. The palace showcases intricate carvings, beautiful domes, arches, and towers, reflecting the grandeur and opulence of the Wadiyar dynasty. The main entrance, known as the Gombe Thotti or Doll's Pavilion, welcomes visitors with its stunning facade adorned with ornate paintings and designs. Inside, the palace features numerous grand halls, including the Kalyana Mantapa (marriage hall), the Durbar Hall (ceremonial meeting hall), and the Diwan-e-Aam (public audience hall), each embellished with intricate craftsmanship and breathtaking artwork
  • One of the highlights of the palace is the Golden Throne, also known as the Chinnada Simhasana, which is showcased during the famous Dasara festival. The throne, embellished with gold and precious stones, is a symbol of the royal heritage and represents the rich cultural legacy of Mysore. The palace also houses a remarkable collection of artifacts, including paintings, sculptures, weapons, and jewelry, providing a glimpse into the royal history of the region
  • The palace is illuminated every Sunday and during festivals, presenting a mesmerizing sight for visitors. The annual Dasara festival, celebrated with grandeur and pomp, attracts a significant number of tourists from around the world. The palace is a captivating blend of architectural brilliance, historical significance, and cultural heritage, making it one of the most famous tourist attractions in India. It receives millions of visitors each year, making it second only to the Taj Mahal in terms of popularity
  • In recent years, efforts have been made to preserve and maintain the palace. The government of Karnataka, along with the Archaeological Survey of India, has taken significant steps to restore and protect this architectural marvel for future generations. Virtual tours and interactive exhibits have also been introduced, enabling visitors to experience the palace's grandeur from anywhere in the world.
  • In conclusion, Mysore Palace, with its captivating architecture, rich history, and royal charm, stands as a testament to the grandeur of the Wadiyar dynasty. Its intricate carvings, opulent interiors, and stunning artwork make it a must-visit destination for history enthusiasts, architecture lovers, and tourists from all walks of life. The palace is not only a symbol of Mysore's regal past but also a reflection of India's rich cultural heritage.



Virupaksha Temple, Hampi, Karnataka:

(Located in the UNESCO World Heritage Site of Hampi, the Virupaksha Temple is a historical marvel with beautiful architecture and intricate carvings.)

  • The Virupaksha Temple, located in Hampi, Karnataka, India, is a renowned Hindu temple dedicated to Sri Virupaksha, a form of Lord Shiva. It is part of the Group of Monuments at Hampi, designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The temple has a rich history that dates back to the 7th century, making it one of the oldest and most significant temples in the region
  • The temple was built by Lakkan Dandesha, a chieftain under the rule of Deva Raya II, also known as Prauda Deva Raya, of the Vijayanagara Empire. Hampi, the capital of the empire, was situated on the banks of the Tungabhadra River, and the Virupaksha Temple served as a major center of pilgrimage and worship. Despite the surrounding ruins, the temple has remained intact and continues to be an active place of devotion.
  • Virupaksha Temple is closely associated with the local goddess Pampadevi, who is believed to be the consort of Lord Shiva in this region. The temple complex consists of a sanctum, three ante chambers, a pillared hall, and an open pillared hall. The intricate carvings and delicately carved pillars adorn the temple, showcasing the architectural brilliance of the Vijayanagara period. Surrounding the main temple are additional structures such as a pillared cloister, entrance gateways, courtyards, and smaller shrines.
  • One of the striking features of the Virupaksha Temple is the use of mathematical concepts in its construction and decoration. The temple exhibits repeated patterns that demonstrate the concept of fractals, with the main shape of the temple being triangular. As you look up towards the top of the temple, the patterns divide and repeat themselves, reminiscent of natural wonders like snowflakes.
  • The temple also boasts impressive gopurams (gateways), with the nine-tiered eastern gateway being the largest, standing at 50 meters tall. It provides access to the outer court, which houses several sub-shrines. Another notable gateway, the Kanakagiri gopura, leads to a small enclosure with subsidiary shrines and eventually to the Tungabhadra River. The temple's proximity to the river is significant, as a narrow channel of the Tungabhadra flows along the temple's terrace, adding to its serene ambiance.
  • Krishnadevaraya, a renowned king of the Vijayanagara Empire, made significant contributions to the Virupaksha Temple. He added the central pillared hall, known for its ornate architecture, and the gateway tower that grants access to the inner courtyard. Inscriptions next to the pillared hall commemorate his contributions and reveal that he commissioned the hall in 1510 AD to mark his accession. The temple halls were used for various purposes, including hosting special programs of music, dance, drama, and deity marriages.
  • The temple is an active place of worship, and it continues to draw huge crowds during the betrothal and marriage festivities of Virupaksha and Pampa, which take place in December. Additionally, an annual chariot festival is celebrated at the temple every February, attracting devotees and tourists alike.
  • The Virupaksha Temple in Hampi stands as a testament to the architectural splendor and religious significance of the Vijayanagara Empire. Its rich history, intricate craftsmanship, and cultural heritage make it a must-visit destination for those exploring the ancient wonders of Karnataka.




Taj Mahal, Agra, Uttar Pradesh:

(Taj Mahal is essentially a tomb built in the memory of Shah Jahan's wife, Mumtaz Mahal.)


  • The Taj Mahal, located in Agra, Uttar Pradesh, India, is one of the most renowned and iconic monuments in the world. Built by the Mughal Emperor Shah Jahān in the 17th century, the Taj Mahal is a mausoleum complex that was constructed as a symbol of love and devotion to Shah Jahān's wife, Mumtaz Mahal

  • As a testament to Mughal architecture, the Taj Mahal is an extraordinary blend of Indian, Persian, and Islamic styles. Its harmonious proportions, intricate detailing, and magnificent structure make it a masterpiece of architectural excellence . The complex encompasses various elements, including a main gateway, garden, mosque, jawāb (a building mirroring the mosque), and the mausoleum itself with its four minarets .

  • Construction of the Taj Mahal began in 1632 and took approximately 22 years to complete. Over 20,000 workers from India, Persia, the Ottoman Empire, and Europe were involved in the construction process . The white marble mausoleum is the centerpiece of the complex, reflecting different hues depending on the sunlight or moonlight. Its four identical facades feature impressive arches and smaller chamfered corners with decorative elements.

  • The most striking feature of the Taj Mahal is its central dome, which reaches a height of 240 feet (73 meters) at the tip of its finial. Surrounding the central dome are four smaller domes, creating a visually stunning composition. The interior of the mausoleum is organized around an octagonal marble chamber adorned with intricate low-relief carvings and semiprecious stones, a technique known as pietra dura. Inside the chamber lie the cenotaphs of Mumtaz Mahal and Shah Jahān, enclosed by a delicately crafted filigree marble screen.

  • The Taj Mahal is set within a spacious garden, designed according to classical Mughal principles. The garden features long watercourses, fountains, walking paths, and ornamental trees, all adding to the serene ambiance and providing a breathtaking approach to the mausoleum . The complex also includes twin mosque buildings situated symmetrically on either side of the mausoleum, contributing to the overall balance and aesthetic appeal .

  • Over the centuries, the Taj Mahal has faced neglect and decay, but significant restoration efforts have been undertaken to preserve this architectural marvel. Restoration projects have been carried out to address the effects of air pollution, such as damage to the marble facade caused by emissions from nearby factories and vehicles . Steps have been taken to reduce pollution and create a buffer zone around the monument to protect its integrity .

  • Today, the Taj Mahal continues to captivate visitors from around the world with its timeless beauty and historical significance. It has been designated as a UNESCO World Heritage site since 1983 and remains a symbol of eternal love, architectural brilliance, and India's rich cultural heritage .







Red Fort, Delhi:

(Constructed by Emperor Shah Jahan, the Red Fort is a magnificent fort complex that served as the residence of Mughal emperors. It is known for its impressive red sandstone walls and architectural grandeur. The fort is a significant historical landmark and hosts the Prime Minister's address on Independence Day )

  • The Red Fort, also known as Lal Qila or Lal Kila, is a historic fort located in Old Delhi, India. Built by the Mughal Emperor Shah Jahān in the 17th century, it stands as a remarkable architectural marvel and a significant tourist attraction
  • Constructed using red sandstone, the fort's massive walls rise to a height of 75 feet (23 meters) and enclose a complex of palaces, entertainment halls, balconies, baths, canals, and beautiful gardens . The fort's design incorporates a blend of Persian, Indian, and European architectural styles, reflecting the rich cultural influences of the Mughal Empire .
  • Among the notable structures within the Red Fort complex is the Hall of Public Audience, also known as the Diwan-i-ʿAm. This grand hall features 60 red sandstone pillars that support a flat roof, creating an impressive architectural spectacle . This is where the emperor would address the public and conduct various official functions.
  • Another prominent structure is the Hall of Private Audience, or the Diwan-i-Khas, which is relatively smaller compared to the Diwan-i-ʿAm. This hall is adorned with intricate designs and a pavilion made of white marble . It served as a private audience chamber for the emperor, where he would meet with his close advisors and hold private meetings
  • The Red Fort also includes an ornate mosque within its premises. This mosque showcases the architectural brilliance of the Mughal era, featuring intricate carvings and designs . The fort's complex layout, with its palaces, halls, and gardens, creates a captivating ambiance that offers a glimpse into the opulence and grandeur of the Mughal court.
  • In recognition of its historical and cultural significance, the Red Fort was designated as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2007 . It stands as a symbol of India's rich heritage and serves as a reminder of the grandeur of the Mughal Empire.
  • Today, the Red Fort continues to attract visitors from around the world who come to admire its architectural splendor and learn about its historical importance. The fort is also famous for hosting the annual flag hoisting ceremony on India's Independence Day, attracting a large crowd of spectators .





Qutub Minar, Delhi:

(Standing at 240 feet tall, the Qutub Minar is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the tallest brick minarets in the world. It was built by Qutb-ud-din Aibak and showcases Indo-Islamic architectural styles. The complex also includes the Quwwat-ul-Islam mosque )

  • The Qutub Minar, also spelled Qutab Minar, is a magnificent minaret and "victory tower" located in the Qutb complex in the Mehrauli area of South Delhi, India. Standing at a height of 72.5 meters (238 feet), it is the tallest tower in India . The minaret is constructed with red and buff sandstone, featuring intricate architectural details.
  • Qutb-ud-Din Aibak, the first ruler of the Delhi Sultanate, initiated the construction of the Qutub Minar in 1199 AD. He laid the foundation and raised the first storey. Subsequently, his son-in-law, Shamsu'd-Din Iltutmish, added three more storeys to the minaret. Each storey is surrounded by a projecting balcony adorned with stone brackets decorated in a honeycomb design, particularly prominent on the first storey.
  • The Qutub Minar bears numerous inscriptions in Arabic and Nagari characters, providing insights into its history. The inscriptions reveal that the minaret underwent repairs and restorations by Firuz Shah Tughlaq in the 14th century and Sikandar Lodi in the 15th century. Major R. Smith also contributed to its restoration in 1829.
  • Adjacent to the Qutub Minar, you will find the Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque, one of the earliest surviving mosques built by the Delhi Sultans. Qutb-ud-Din Aibak constructed this mosque in 1198 AD, repurposing the carved columns and architectural elements from 27 Hindu and Jain temples that were demolished for its construction. The mosque complex also features the Iron Pillar, an ancient artifact with a Sanskrit inscription dating back to the 4th century AD.
  • The Qutub Minar is not only a remarkable architectural structure but also a symbol of the fusion between Islamic and local South Asian influences. Its design showcases a synthesis of Islamic architectural principles and regional elements, such as Indic temple motifs. This fusion reflects the historical and cultural exchange that took place during its construction.
  • Today, the Qutub Minar stands as a testament to Delhi's rich history and architectural heritage. It attracts visitors from around the world who come to admire its towering presence, intricate craftsmanship, and the historical significance of the Qutub complex.





Humayun's Tomb, Delhi:

(Commissioned by Emperor Akbar, Humayun's Tomb is a splendid example of Mughal architecture and served as a model for the construction of the Taj Mahal. The tomb is known for its red sandstone structure and beautiful geometric patterns )

  • Humayun's Tomb is a magnificent architectural masterpiece located in Delhi, India. It serves as the tomb of Emperor Humayun, the second Mughal emperor of India. Commissioned in 1558 by Empress Bega Begum, Humayun's first wife and chief consort, the tomb was designed by Persian architects Mirak Mirza Ghiyas and Sayyid Muhammad . It is situated in Nizamuddin East, Delhi, close to the Dina-panah Citadel, also known as Purana Qila (Old Fort).
  • The tomb is notable for being the first garden-tomb on the Indian subcontinent . Its construction marked a significant leap in Mughal architecture and set a precedent for future Mughal mausoleums, including the iconic Taj Mahal. The tomb complex features the main tomb of Emperor Humayun, as well as several smaller monuments along the pathway leading to it. These include the tomb complex of Isa Khan Niyazi, an Afghan noble from Sher Shah Suri's court, constructed in 1547.
  • Humayun's Tomb is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, recognized for its cultural significance and architectural grandeur. It blends elements of Persian and Indian architectural styles, showcasing intricate craftsmanship and the innovative use of red sandstone on a large scale. The tomb complex also houses the graves of Empress Bega Begum, Dara Shikoh (Humayun's great-great-grandson and son of Emperor Shah Jahan), and several other Mughal rulers.
  • Over the years, the tomb has undergone extensive restoration work to preserve its historical and architectural integrity. It remains a popular tourist attraction, drawing visitors with its splendid design, serene gardens, and its significance as a symbol of Mughal architecture and heritage.



Agra Fort, Agra, Uttar Pradesh:

(Originally built in the 11th century, the Agra Fort was transformed into a magnificent palace-fortress by Emperor Akbar. It features impressive walls, gates, and palaces made of red sandstone. The fort offers stunning views of the Taj Mahal from its premises. )

  • Agra Fort, also known as Agra's Red Fort, is a historical fort located in the city of Agra, Uttar Pradesh, India. It was built during the Mughal era and served as the main residence of the Mughal emperors until 1638, when the capital was shifted to Delhi. The fort was initially constructed by Sikandar Lodi in the 15th century, and later renovated by Emperor Akbar in the 16th century. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is often overshadowed by its sister monument, the Taj Mahal, as both are located in close proximity.
  • The fort covers an area of 94 acres and features impressive architecture, including massive circular bastions, double ramparts, and grand gates such as the Delhi Gate and the Lahore Gate . Within the fort, visitors can explore various structures, including the Diwan-i-Am (Hall of Public Audience) and the Royal Pavilions. The fort offers breathtaking views of the Yamuna River and the Taj Mahal from its balconies, providing a captivating experience for visitors .
  • Over the years, Agra Fort has witnessed significant historical events, including battles and changes in power. It was occupied by different rulers, including the Mughals, Marathas, and British. The fort also played a role during the Indian Rebellion of 1857, marking the end of the British East India Company's rule in India .
  • Today, Agra Fort stands as a testament to the grandeur and architectural brilliance of the Mughal dynasty. Its historical significance, impressive structures, and panoramic views make it a popular tourist attraction, attracting visitors from around the world.

Konark Sun Temple, Odisha:

(Dedicated to the Sun God, the Konark Sun Temple is a UNESCO World Heritage Site renowned for its architectural splendor. The temple's unique chariot-like structure and intricate stone carvings make it a marvel of Indian architecture.)

  • The Konark Sun Temple, located in the state of Odisha, India, is a renowned Hindu temple dedicated to the sun god Surya. Built around 1250 CE by King Narasimhadeva I of the Eastern Ganga dynasty, the temple resembles a giant stone chariot with twelve wheels. It showcases exquisite sculptures and artwork on various themes, demonstrating the mastery of Hindu temple architecture.
  • The temple's design follows the Kalinga or Orissa style of architecture, characterized by a square ground plan, a curvilinear tower (shikhara), and ornate external projections known as rathas or pagas. Despite parts being in ruins, the temple continues to attract tourists and pilgrims.


Khajuraho Group of Monuments, Madhya Pradesh:

(The Khajuraho Temples, built between the 9th and 11th centuries, are renowned for their exquisite sculptures and intricate artwork. These temples, known for their depiction of love and eroticism, showcase the architectural brilliance of the Chandela dynasty.)

  • The temples at Khajuraho were built during the Chandella dynasty, which reached its apogee between 950 and 1050. Only about 20 temples remain; they fall into three distinct groups and belong to two different religions Hinduism and Jainism. They strike a perfect balance between architecture and sculpture. The Temple of Kandariya is decorated with a profusion of sculptures that are among the greatest masterpieces of Indian art.
  • The group of temples of Khajuraho testifies to the culmination of northern Indian temple art and architecture of the Chandella dynasty who ruled the region in the 10th and 11th centuries CE. Distributed over an area of 6 square km in a picturesque landscape, the 23 temples (including one partly excavated structure) that form the western, eastern, and southern clusters of the Khajuraho Group of Monuments are rare surviving examples that display the originality and high quality of Nagara-style temple architecture.
  • The Khajuraho Group of Monuments demonstrates in layout and physical form, the pinnacle of temple architectural development in northern India. Built in sandstone, each temple is elevated from its environs by a highly ornate terraced platform, or jagati, on which stands the body, or jangha, whose sanctum is topped by a tower, or shikhara, of a type unique to Nagara, where the verticality of the principal spire atop the sanctum is accentuated by a series of miniature spires flanking it, each symbolizing Mount Kailasa, the abode of the Gods.
  • The plan of the temples shows the spatial hierarchy of axially aligned interconnected spaces. The temples are entered through an ornate entrance porch (ardhamandapa), which leads to the main hall (mandapa), through which one accesses the vestibule (antarala) before reaching the sanctum (garbhagriha). The main halls of the temples were often accompanied by lateral transepts with projecting windows as well as a circumambulatory path around the sanctum. Larger temples had an additional pair of transepts and were accompanied by subsidiary shrines on the four corners of its jagati.

Sanchi Stupa, Madhya Pradesh:

(Constructed by Emperor Ashoka in the 3rd century BC, the Sanchi Stupa is one of the oldest stone structures in India. This Buddhist monument is famous for its intricate carvings and the Great Stupa, which contains relics of Lord Buddha.)

  • Sanchi Stupa, located in Madhya Pradesh, India, is an ancient Buddhist monument and one of the oldest stone structures in the country. Built in the 3rd century BCE by Emperor Ashoka, it is a hemispherical brick structure housing relics of the Buddha. Over time, it was enlarged, adorned with gateways and railings, and reached its final form in the 1st century BCE. The Great Stupa stands 54 feet high and 120 feet wide, featuring intricate relief sculptures depicting the life of the Buddha and auspicious symbols
  • Sanchi Stupa is one of the oldest stone structures in India built during the Mauryan period. The sculptures, monuments and lush gardens of Sanchi Stupa have been designated as the World Heritage site by UNESCO
  • Sanchi, a small village is located at the foothill very close to the capital city of Madhya Pradesh, Bhopal. The place attracts wanderers from across the world with its ancient Stupas, monasteries and prosperous Buddhist culture. The Great Stupa at Sanchi is one of the best-preserved Stupas with its ornamental gateways attracting thousands of pilgrims and travellers.
  • Sanchi Stupa was commissioned by Emperor Ashoka and supervised by his queen Devi and daughter Vidisha. After the emperor accepted Buddhism, he built the first stupa at Sanchi followed by several more in the later years. Sanchi Stupa remained deserted and undiscovered from the 14th century until the year 1818 when General Taylor rediscovered the site.
  • Sir John Marshall established an archaeological museum here in 1919, which is today known as Sanchi Museum. The pillar of Sanchi Stupa has an Ashokan inscription called Schism Edict. It also has an inscription in the ornamental Sankha Lipi from the Gupta period. It’s the oldest stone structure in India. Sanchi Stupa has a large number of Brahmi inscriptions. Currently, this Stupa is 36.5 m in diameter and 21.64 m high.
  • The place possesses historical importance, however, Lord Buddha never visited this place. The famous Ashoka pillar comprising of four lions is also found in Sanchi. These pillars are constructed in a Greco-Buddhist style. Sanchi Stupa architecture was declared as the world heritage in the year 1989.




Hampi Monuments, Karnataka:

( Hampi is a UNESCO World Heritage Site that houses the remains of the Vijayanagara Empire. The site includes several temples, palaces, and other structures, showcasing the architectural grandeur of the empire.
